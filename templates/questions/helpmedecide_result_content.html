What is a sole trader?

The sole trader structure is the most common business structure in Australia. It’s the cheapest and easiest to set up, and generally suits people who:

  • don’t want to do extra tax reporting
  • want to use any business losses to offset their other personal income
  • want to claim business expenses to reduce their personal income
  • have a low risk of going into significant business debt

Pros

  • Cheapest structure
  • Simple to set up and run
  • No additional reporting
  • All profits belong to you
  • You have complete control
  • Easy to change to a different structure
  • Easy to sell or close your business

Cons

  • You risk losing your personal assets if your business goes into debt
  • You’re responsible for all taxes and losses
  • You’re responsible for organising your own super
  • You’re not an employee of your business, so you don’t get paid annual or sick leave

What is a company?

A company structure means your business is a separate legal entity from you. This means that it can borrow money and be sued like a person.

A company has at least one owner (shareholder) and one director. You can be both.

Companies are expensive and complicated to set up, and generally suit people who:

  • expect their business income to be highly variable, and want the option to use losses to offset future profits
  • think their business has a high risk of going into debt, and want to limit the risk of losing personal assets such as their home (limit their liability).

It’s important to know that companies must meet complex reporting and legal requirements.

Pros

  • Limited liability for owners
  • Limited liability for directors, if they meet their legal obligations
  • Ability to raise money by offering company shares
  • Profits can be invested back into the company or distributed to owners
  • Easy to sell or pass on to another owner
  • Can carry losses forward to offset future profits

Cons

  • Expensive and complicated to set up
  • Complex reporting and legal requirements
  • Can’t distribute losses
  • Limited or no control of company affairs

What is a partnership?

A partnership is where two or more people own a business together and share the income and losses. It’s relatively inexpensive and easy to set up and operate, and generally suits people who:

  • want to minimise their reporting requirements
  • want to use any business losses to offset their other personal income
  • want to claim business expenses to reduce their personal income
  • have low risk of going into significant business debt

Pros

  • Relatively cheap to set up
  • Simple to set up and run
  • Minimal reporting requirements
  • Your share of the profits belong to you
  • Partners combine skills, experience and investment

Cons

  • You risk losing your personal assets if your business goes into debt, even if your partner was responsible
  • You’re responsible for organising your own super
  • You’re not an employee of your business, so you don’t get paid annual or sick leave
  • Sharing business decisions can sometimes result in disputes

What is a trust?

In a trust structure, a trustee holds your business for the benefit of others (the beneficiaries).

A trustee can be a person or a company, and is responsible for everything in the trust, including income and losses.

Trust structures are expensive and complicated to set up, and are generally used to protect the business assets for beneficiaries. The trustee decides how business profits should be distributed to the beneficiaries.

If profits are invested back into the business, the business must pay penalty tax rates on the profits.

Pros

  • Business assets are protected
  • Limited liability – especially if the trustee is a company
  • Flexibility of asset and income distribution

Cons

  • Expensive and complicated to set up
  • Difficult to end or change the trust once it’s been set up
  • Pay penalty rates on any profits invested back into the business
