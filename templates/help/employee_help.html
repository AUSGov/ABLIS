Employees

Generally, employees:

  • are supervised by an employer
  • are required to carry out their work in a particular way
  • earn a wage or salary
  • are entitled to paid leave
  • work set hours on a permanent, fixed term or casual basis
  • can't subcontract work tasks given to them.

Don't confuse employees with contractors! Contractors set up businesses to hire out their services to other businesses, and can negotiate their own working arrangements.

Fringe benefits

Other common fringe benefits include offering:

  • cheap loans
  • free private health insurance
  • food or drink, such as meals and cocktail parties
  • entertainment such as sporting events, sightseeing tours, movie tickets and harbour cruises
  • low interest or interest-free loans
  • accommodation rent-free or at a reduced rent.

PAYG Withholding

When you have employees (this includes company directors), you must withhold tax amounts from the wage or salary payments you make to employees, and forward these tax amounts to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

PAYG Withholding isn't a tax on your business - it's a tax on your employees' income that you help the ATO to collect.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for PAYG Withholding means you:

  • work out how much you need to withhold
  • report PAYG Withholding amounts to the ATO
  • pay PAYG Withholding amounts to the ATO when they're due
  • include PAYG Withholding amounts in your employees' pay slips and payment summaries.

Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT)

As an employer, if you give certain fringe benefits to your employees, you must pay tax on the value of the fringe benefits.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for FBT means you:

  • work out how much FBT you have to pay
  • keep records related to the fringe benefits you provide
  • report fringe benefits on your employees' payment summaries
  • report fringe benefits to the Australian Taxation Ofice (ATO)
  • pay the FBT to the ATO.
