Australian Business Number (ABN)

If you run a business in Australia, you should have an ABN.

An ABN is a unique 11 digit number that identifies your business to the public, the Australian Taxation Office (ATO), and other government agencies. Applying for an ABN is free.

Why should I have an ABN?

Having an ABN means you can:

  • confirm your business identity to others when ordering and invoicing
  • avoid having business clients withhold 46.5% of any payments they make to you
  • register a business name
  • register an Australian domain name.

You also need to have an ABN if you register for:

  • Goods and Services Tax (GST)
  • Pay As You Go (PAYG) withholding
  • Fuel Tax Credits (FTC)
  • Wine Equalisation Tax (WET)
  • Luxury Car Tax (LCT)

Business Tax File Number (TFN)

All partnerships and companies need a business TFN to deal with the Australian Taxation Office (ATO), for example to report business income and deductions.

Sole traders can use their individual TFN for both their personal and business matters.

Find out more about TFNs for businesses on the ATO website.

Company registration

All companies need to be registered if they’re operating in Australia. The cost of registering a company depends on the type of company. The most common company structure, a proprietary limited company, costs $463 to register.

Before you register

You’ll need to:

  • decide whether your company will be limited or unlimited
  • decide on the officeholders (directors and secretaries)
  • get written consent from the officeholders and the shareholders
  • choose a company name (that is, the company’s legal name)
  • decide what rules will govern your company
  • understand your legal obligations.

When you’ve registered

When registered, the company will get:

  • an Australian Company Number (ACN)
  • a Certificate of Registration.

Find out more

Read starting a company on the Australian Securities & Investments Commission (ASIC) website.

Business name

When you register a business name, you register it nationally with the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC). This means you can operate your business in any state or territory in Australia.

Find out more about registering your business name on the ASIC website.

Protecting your business name

Registering a business name doesn't give you exclusive use! To stop others using your business name, you need to apply for a trade mark on the IP Australia website.

PAYG Withholding

When you have employees, you must withhold tax amounts from the wage or salary payments you make to employees, and forward these tax amounts to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

PAYG Withholding isn't a tax on your business - it's a tax on your employees' income that you help the ATO to collect.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for PAYG Withholding means you:

  • work out how much you need to withhold
  • report PAYG Withholding amounts to the ATO
  • pay PAYG Withholding amounts to the ATO when they're due
  • include PAYG Withholding amounts in your employees' pay slips and payment summaries.

Fringe Benefits Tax (FBT)

As an employer, if you give certain fringe benefits to your employees, you must pay tax on the value of the fringe benefits.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for FBT means you:

  • work out how much FBT you have to pay
  • keep records related to the fringe benefits you provide
  • report fringe benefits on your employees' payment summaries
  • report fringe benefits to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO)
  • pay the FBT to the ATO.

Goods & Services Tax (GST)

It's recommended you register for GST if you've just started a business and expect to have business turnover of $75,000 or more in your first year.

You must register for GST if:

  • your business has a turnover of $75,000 or more
  • your business charges passengers for taxi services or limousine travel
  • you want to claim Fuel Tax Credits (FTC).

Once you register for GST, you must stay registered for at least 12 months.

How GST works

GST is a tax of 10% on most goods and services sold in Australia. Generally, businesses that are registered for GST:

  • collect GST on the goods and services they sell and forward it to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO)
  • claim credits for the GST included in the price of goods and services they buy for their business.

What do I need to do?

If you're registered for GST, you'll need to:

  • work out which goods and services you sell are subject to GST
  • include GST in the prices you charge for those goods and services
  • give tax invoices to your customers that include the GST amount
  • account for GST in your books
  • put aside the GST you've collected
  • get tax invoices and claim GST credits for goods and services you buy for your own business
  • lodge activity statements or annual returns to the ATO
  • pay GST to the ATO (or get a GST refund).

Find out more

Read about how GST works on the ATO website.

Luxury Car Tax (LCT)

LCT is a tax on the supply and import of cars that exceed the LCT threshold.

You must register for LCT if:

  • you're registered for GST, and
  • you sell or import luxury cars.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for LCT means you:

  • work out the LCT payable and include it in the import or sale
  • account for LCT sales and imports in your books
  • report and pay LCT to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

Find out more

Read about LCT on the ATO website.

Fuel Tax Credits (FTC)

If you register for FTC, you can claim credits for the fuel tax you pay on these fuels. The amount of fuel tax credits you can claim depends on the type of fuel you use and what business activity you use it in.

What do I need to do?

Being registered for FTC means you:

  • work out how much you can claim on eligible fuels
  • keep records of the type and quantity of fuels, when you bought the fuels and how you used them in your business.

Am I eligible?

To be eligible, you must:

  • be registered for GST
  • use eligible fuels.

Try the FTC eligibility tool on the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) website.

Wine Equalisation Tax (WET)

You must register for WET if:

  • you're registered for GST and
  • you import or produce wine or sell wines wholesale, where the wines are subject to WET.

What is WET?

WET is a tax on the consumption of wine in Australia, and it's usually applied to the last wholesale sale - this is usually the sale to the retailer. You collect the tax to give to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

What do I need to do?

Being registered for WET means you:

  • work out which sales are subject to WET
  • issue tax invoices that show the WET amount
  • claim any WET credits you may be entitled to (including rebates or refunds)
  • account for WET amounts in your books
  • put aside WET you've collected
  • report WET to the ATO.

Find out more

Read about WET on the ATO website.

Goods & Services Tax (GST)

You don't have to register for GST if you:

  • don't expect to have a turnover of $75,000 or more in your first year
  • don't charge passengers for taxi services
  • don't charge passengers for limousine travel.

What is GST?

GST is a tax of 10% on most goods and services sold in Australia. The money raised by GST is given to the state and territory governments, to provide public services to their communities.
